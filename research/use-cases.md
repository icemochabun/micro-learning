# Micro-learning Real Use Cases — Research Summary

**Last updated:** 2026-03-18
**Purpose:** Evidence base for Phase 1 pilot — care staff, seniors, volunteers in social service / senior care context
**Singapore focus:** AAC staff, AIC Learning Network, Age Well SG

---

## Audience 1: Senior Learners

### Use case 1.1 — Medication adherence via micro-learning (RCT, 2025)
- **Source:** [Scientific Reports — Microlearning-based education on medication adherence in the elderly](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-29769-7)
- **Study:** Randomised controlled trial, 118 elderly participants, 2024
- **Intervention:** Short video clips (microlearning format) vs standard care
- **Outcome:** Significant improvement in medication adherence and health literacy in the intervention group
- **Relevance:** Direct evidence that micro-learning video clips work for senior health behaviour change — transferable to AAC health self-management modules

### Use case 1.2 — Digital literacy via micro-sessions with peer support (2024)
- **Source:** [JMIR Aging — Digital Literacy Training via Community-Engaged Learning](https://aging.jmir.org/2024/1/e51675)
- **Study:** 18 older adults + 27 undergraduates, 8-week program, weekly 1-hour sessions at a senior centre
- **Outcome:** Increased digital literacy skills, confidence in technology use, improved attitudes toward own aging; students' comfort with older adults increased
- **Relevance:** Peer/intergenerational facilitation model can be adapted for volunteers supporting senior learners in AAC digital upskilling

### Use case 1.3 — Digital literacy program sustainability (2025, based on 2023–24 data)
- **Source:** [Springer — Effective but sustainable? Digital literacy program for older adults](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10639-025-13364-4)
- **Study:** Regional library, 3 implementation cycles, surveys + interviews with learners, instructors, administrator
- **Outcome:** Enhanced confidence across digital literacy domains; challenges: limited funding, volunteer instructor recruitment, space/device availability
- **Relevance:** Real implementation barriers to plan for — device access, instructor capacity, marketing

### Design rules confirmed by evidence
| Rule | Basis |
|---|---|
| 16pt+ fonts, 4.5:1 contrast | Cognitive/sensory decline in aging; WCAG AA standard |
| Single-tap navigation | Motor decline; peer study showed higher completion with simplified interfaces |
| Concrete images + text labels | No abstract icons — seniors interpret literal imagery better |
| Short (<5 min), one idea per screen | Working memory limits in older adults (Sweller CLT) |
| Facilitated first experience | Peer/intergenerational support significantly improves adoption |

## Audience 2: Senior Care Staff (AAC Staff / Deskless Workers)

### Use case 2.1 — App-based microlearning for older adult care personnel (2023)
- **Source:** [JMIR Medical Education — Digital Microlearning for Older Adult Care Personnel](https://mededu.jmir.org/2023/1/e45177) | [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10728783/)
- **Study:** 26 staff (22 operational, 4 managerial) across 3 older adult care organisations; 15–20 min courses over ~1 month; topics: COVID-19 hygiene and dementia care
- **Intervention:** Minnity Learning Platform; 2–3 minute modules with self-assessment tests; mobile/computer access; flexible scheduling
- **Outcomes:** Staff rated app as "simple to use" and "well integrated"; majority agreed courses were "valuable and beneficial"; high perceived knowledge retention; confidence in applying knowledge to work performance; younger/less experienced staff rated benefits more highly
- **Caveat:** Complex ethical topics (e.g., end-of-life decisions) better suited to discussion-based training
- **Relevance:** Direct model for AAC care staff — 2–3 min modules, dementia topics, flexible mobile access aligns exactly with CLAUDE.md constraints

### Use case 2.2 — Microlearning for CNAs in skilled nursing facilities (2021)
- **Source:** [PMC — Research Needed on Microlearning for CNAs in Skilled Nursing Facilities](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8415732/)
- **Key findings:**
  - Microlearning provides flexibility for deskless staff to learn without leaving the care floor
  - Reduces cost of training by eliminating backfill during classroom sessions
  - Supports implementation of person-centred care protocols on the floor
  - Can serve as a refresher before performing infrequently-used skills (safer than memory-only)
- **Relevance:** Cost and operational argument for micro-learning in AAC context; falls prevention and PPE protocols are ideal refresher candidates

### Use case 2.3 — Microlearning improves clinical safety and performance (scoping review)
- **Source:** [PMC — Microlearning in Health Professions Education: Scoping Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6683654/)
- **Key findings:** Microlearning improves clinical performance and increases safety; effective as refresher for infrequent skills; consistently outperforms passive reading in retention
- **Relevance:** Evidence base for clinical protocol modules (delirium, falls, PPE, medication side effects)

### Use case 2.4 — Singapore AAC context: AIC Learning Network
- **Source:** [AIC Learning Network](https://aic.sg/partners/aic-learning-network) | [CCSSF for AACs](https://www.aic.sg/partners/mosaic/community-care-skills-standards-framework-active-ageing-centres/)
- **Context:** AIC-LN offers 360+ courses (92 free e-courseware) via the Community Care Learning Management System (CCLMS) for in-service CCO staff; 8 approved Learning Institutes
- **2024 update:** Refreshed Community Care Skills Standards Framework (CCSSF) specifically for AAC staff as part of Age Well SG; endorsed training roadmap covering clinical, behavioural, and psychosocial skills
- **Platform:** CCLMS (hosted on Wizlearn LMS) — existing infrastructure that micro-learning content could be delivered through
- **Gap identified:** Current offering is LMS-based courses, not true micro-learning (2–10 min, mobile-first). An AAC-specific micro-learning layer would be additive

### Recommended Phase 1 module topics (from evidence + AIC/CCSSF priority areas)
1. Delirium early signs — high-stakes, observable criteria, Template A candidate
2. Fall prevention — common, protocol-driven, easily chunked
3. Dementia communication — already validated in JMIR 2023 study (topic used in pilot)
4. PPE donning/doffing — procedural, visual, ideal for short video + checklist format
5. Resident dignity & consent — soft skills, Template B scenario candidate

## Audience 3: Volunteers

### Use case 3.1 — Micro-volunteering as onboarding strategy
- **Source:** [The Class Consulting Group — 8 Tips to Create an Effective Volunteer Onboarding Program](https://www.theclassconsultinggroup.org/post/8-tips-to-create-an-effective-volunteer-onboarding-program)
- **Key findings:**
  - Structured onboarding (concise agenda, online modules, role-specific content) boosts volunteer confidence and retention
  - Welcome video + mentor introduction makes onboarding feel personal
  - Automated, recorded modules allow scale without staff burden
  - Micro-volunteering tasks (low commitment, flexible) allow volunteers to experience impact before committing — reduces no-shows
- **Relevance:** Phase model for volunteer onboarding (Admin → Culture → Task) maps directly to Template C structure

### Use case 3.2 — Engagement and retention drivers
- **Source:** [VolunteerMatters — Volunteer Retention Strategies 2025](https://www.volunteermatters.com/blog/volunteer-retention-strategies) | [BetterImpact — Mastering Volunteer Management](https://blog.betterimpact.com/en/volunteer-management)
- **Key findings:**
  - Interest-based role matching is the single strongest predictor of retention
  - Mission impact stories + peer recognition drive long-term commitment more than annual events
  - Visual progress trackers, milestone badges, and gamification increase engagement
  - Continued learning opportunities (post-onboarding) reinforce belonging and capability
- **Relevance:** Volunteer UI design should foreground: mission story, progress tracker, badge milestones

### Use case 3.3 — Literature review: volunteer management practices (AmeriCorps)
- **Source:** [AmeriCorps — Engaging Volunteers: Comprehensive Literature Review](https://www.americorps.gov/sites/default/files/document/Literature%20Review%20for%20Volunteer%20Management_0.pdf)
- **Key findings:** Training, support, and interest-matching are positively associated with retention; volunteers need to see personal benefit; skills development + social connection + impact visibility are core retention levers
- **Relevance:** Framework for volunteer module content priorities

### Recommended volunteer onboarding sequence
| Phase | Content | Format | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 — Admin | Role overview, policies, safety basics | Short text + checklist | 5 min |
| Phase 2 — Culture | Organisation mission, impact story, values | Video + reflection prompt | 5 min |
| Phase 3 — Task | Role-specific skills, scenario | Scenario/branching | 7 min |
| Ongoing | Monthly micro-updates, recognition milestones | Push notification + badge | 2 min |

## Cross-cutting findings

| Finding | Implication |
|---|---|
| WhatsApp/SMS: 98% adoption in low-resource settings | Priority delivery channel for all 3 audiences in remote or low-bandwidth AAC contexts |
| 80–90% completion for micro-learning vs 20–30% for traditional | Strong ROI case for leadership buy-in |
| Spaced repetition (Day 0 → 7 → 30) significantly outperforms one-off training | Build spacing into module delivery schedule from Phase 1 |
| Confidence gains are the most consistently reported outcome across all studies | Frame module impact messaging around "feeling more confident at work" |
| Complex ethical/relational topics require discussion, not solo micro-learning | Hybrid model: micro-learning for protocol/fact recall, facilitated sessions for ethics/soft skills |
