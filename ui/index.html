HOOK · 30 SEC

Mr Tan, 82, was his usual cheerful self at breakfast. By lunch he doesn't recognise his daughter.

This is delirium — an acute, sudden change in awareness and cognition. Most staff miss the early signs. You won't, after this module.

⏱ This module takes about 3 minutes. One idea. One skill. One quiz.

Scenario · 3 min

CORE · 90 SEC

3 early signs of delirium

  1. Sudden confusion — disoriented to time or place out of nowhere
  2. Unusual quietness — withdrawn, not responding as usual
  3. Agitation — restless, picking at clothing, calling out repeatedly
Key word: sudden. Delirium is acute — gradual memory loss is different.

Branching · 4 min

SCENARIO

Mrs Lee, 78, opens the door when you arrive. She doesn't make eye contact. She steps aside to let you in — but doesn't say anything.

You've met her once before during orientation. What do you do?

Branching · 4 min

CONSEQUENCE — PATH A

Mrs Lee stiffens slightly. "I'm fine," she says, and looks away. The visit feels stilted — she answers in single words for most of the hour.

Asking about emotions too soon — before trust is built — can feel intrusive. She needed a moment to settle first.

Branching · 4 min

CONSEQUENCE — PATH B

"Good morning, Mrs Lee — lovely to see you again." You wait. After a moment, she smiles. "Come, sit. I made some tea."

A warm, unhurried greeting gave her time to feel safe. Patience is the skill.

Branching · 4 min

ANCHOR

Let them set the pace.

Your job on arrival is to create safety — not to diagnose the mood. Seniors who feel unhurried open up naturally.

Your one action: when you arrive next time, give a warm greeting then wait quietly for 5 seconds before saying anything else.

Reference · 1 min

1 of 3
